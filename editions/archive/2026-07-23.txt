TRIVIA THURSDAY

George Vanderbilt II was 26 years old when he bought the first parcel of what would become Biltmore Estate in 1888. He had never built a house before. He hired Richard Morris Hunt — who had designed the pedestal for the Statue of Liberty — and told him to design something in the style of a Loire Valley château. The result took six years and required its own brick factory, woodworking shop, and a private railroad spur to haul Indiana limestone to the construction site.

Vanderbilt spent his winters there until his death in 1914. The house opened to the public in 1930 — the family needed the revenue during the Depression — and it remains the largest privately-owned home in America. THE CULTURE FILE

Biltmore's Banquet Hall is 72 feet long with a triple fireplace and flags from the world's naval powers — Vanderbilt's nod to his family's shipping fortune. The house holds 43 bathrooms, which was unheard of in 1895. F. Scott Fitzgerald stayed there twice in the 1930s while Zelda was a patient nearby; some scholars believe the estate inspired parts of *The Last Tycoon*. In 1942, during the war, the National Gallery sent 62 masterpieces from Washington — including Gilbert Stuart's *George Washington* — to Biltmore's Music Room for safekeeping. The paintings stayed there until 1944.
